The push for a COVID-19 vaccine

By World Health Organization (WHO)

The world is in the midst of a COVID-19 pandemic. As WHO and partners work together on the response -- tracking the pandemic, advising on critical interventions, distributing vital medical supplies to those in need--- they are racing to find a vaccine.

Vaccines save millions of lives each year. Vaccines work by training and preparing the body’s natural defences --- the immune system--- to recognize and fight off the viruses and bacteria they target. If the body is exposed to those disease-causing germs later, the body is immediately ready to destroy them, preventing illness. Immunization currently prevents 2-3 million deaths, every year from diseases like diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, influenza and measles. There are now vaccines to prevent more than 20 life-threatening diseases, and work is ongoing at unprecedented speed to also make COVID-19 a vaccine-preventable disease.

Prevention and Protection from COVID-19








How can COVID-19 be prevented?

Reduce your chances of being infected by or spreading COVID-19 by:

Updated as of April 10, 2020

1. Observing physical distancing

Stay at home to reduce interaction with other people, and maintain at least a one meter distance (about 2 arms length) from people around you both inside and outside home.


2. Practicing hand hygiene

Wash your hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds, or use an alcohol-based hand rub.


3. Following cough etiquette

Cover your mouth and nose with a bent elbow or tissue when you sneeze or cough.


4. Avoid touching mouth, eyes, nose

Do not touch your mouth, eyes, or nose, especially if you’ve touched dirty surfaces.


5. Cleaning and disinfecting

Keep your home and your belongings clean by spraying or wiping them down with 7 tablespoons (100 mL) of bleach mixed with one liter of water, or with a 70% alcohol solution.


6. Following health officers’ instructions

Health officers will have the most up-to-date information on COVID-19. Listen to their advice.


How do I properly wash my hands?

Washing your hands is easy.

Doing it correctly and regularly is one of the most effective ways to prevent yourself from getting sick and the virus from spreading.
Remember: Do it for 20 seconds or more. Need a timer? Sing "Happy Birthday" twice or the chorus of your favorite song.

Updated as of April 10, 2020

Properly wash your hands by following the steps below:

1. Wet your hands with water and apply soap.


2. Lather your hands by rubbing your palms together with soap.


3. Soap the back of your hands.


4. Scrub in between your fingers.


5. Scrub under your nails.


6. Scrub between your thumbs.


7. Scrub around your fingers with a twisting motion.


8. Rinse your hands well under clean water. Dry your hands with a single-use towel or air dryer.

Key times to wash your hands:

★ After you have been in a public place


★ After you have touched an item or surface that may be frequently touched by other people, such as door handles, tables, or shopping carts, etc.


★ Before touching your eyes, nose, or mouth because that’s how the virus enters our bodies.


★ After blowing your nose, coughing, or sneezing


★ Before, during, and after preparing food


★ Before eating food


★ Before and after caring for someone at home who is sick


★ Before and after treating a cut or wound


★ After using the toilet


★ After changing diapers or cleaning up a child who has used the toilet


★ After touching an animal, animal feed, or animal waste


★ After touching garbage

How can I keep the virus away from our home?

Unless it is an emergency or absolutely necessary, do not leave your home.

If you need to get your family basic necessities or you are an essential worker, you can do a few things to prevent COVID-19 from following you home.

Updated as of April 10, 2020

While outside your home:

1. Always bring some alcohol or hand sanitizer. Put it in your bag or pocket. Use it regularly if you cannot wash your hands with soap and water.


2. Do not touch your face.


3. Do not touch anything with your hands, if you can avoid it.


4. Practice physical distancing. Keep 1 meter (2 arms length) or more space from you and other people.

When you get home:

1. Spray the soles of your shoes with alcohol, and remove them before entering the house. Leave your shoes in a designated area outside or near your front door.


2. Remove your clothing and put them in a separate laundry bag. Wash them in water and detergent with some bleach as soon as possible.


3. Leave your bags, keys, spare change, and other items brought outside in a designated area or box near your front door.


4. Clean the surfaces of bags or other items brought outside by spraying them with diluted bleach or 70% alcohol solution.


5. Have alcohol or sanitizer outside your front door, and sanitize your hands before entering. As long as you have not sanitized your hands, try not to touch anything in the house.


6. Take a bath as soon as you get home. Thoroughly wash all exposed areas with soap. As with handwashing, scrub for at least 20 seconds.


7. If you were wearing PPEs such as gloves or masks, throw them away in a separate trash bin and wash your hands thoroughly. Remember to keep PPEs separate from regular trash. Keep them in a sealed bag for 72 hours before leaving them outside to be collected.

Do I need a face mask to protect myself against COVID-19?

The general public when going outside their homes must wear face masks. Wearing a face mask alone does not fully protect you from COVID-19. You should still observe the following measures:

Updated as of April 10, 2020

While outside your home:

1. Maintain a one meter distance (2 arms length) from each other.


2. Cover mouth and nose when coughing or sneezing.


3. Wash hands with soap and water or with an alcohol-based solution.


If you are healthy, it’s okay to use cloth or DIY face masks or medical masks when you leave your home


If you are sick, wear a medical face mask, except when eating or sleeping. Wear a medical face mask when caring for someone who is sick, too.


Preserve the supply of medical masks or N-95 respirators for those who need it the most — healthcare workers, frontliners, and people with COVID-19 symptoms.

How do I properly wear, remove and dispose of face masks?

Updated as of April 10, 2020

Wearing face masks:

1. Make sure your face mask covers your mouth and nose, and is snug on your face.


2. Keep your mask on at all times while in public, even when speaking. Remove it only when you get home or when you need to eat.


3. Do not touch your face while wearing your mask.


4. Do not touch the front of your mask while wearing it to avoid contamination, or the transfer of germs, bacteria, and viruses.


5. Wash your hands with soap and water or an alcohol-based solution before and after fixing or touching your mask.

Removing and disposing face masks:

1. Remove your face mask from behind without touching the front of your mask.


2. Do not re-use disposable masks. Replace them daily, or when they are damaged, soiled, or moist.


3. Wash reusable cloth masks with hot water and detergent. Dry them under direct sunlight.


4. After removing your face mask, clean your hands with soap and water or an alcohol-based solution immediately.


How do I practice physical distancing?

Physical distancing means staying physically distant from others. You minimize close interactions with other people to help limit the spread of the COVID-19 virus. The virus can spread to other people through droplet transmission when an infected person coughs or sneezes. These saliva droplets can go as far as one meter.

Updated as of April 10, 2020

Do's:

1. Do stay at home as much as possible

2. Do stay at least one meter (two arms length) away from others at all times.

3. Do wave or nod at others to greet them

4. Do text, chat or call your friends or family instead of visiting them

Don'ts:

1. Don't go to crowded places and non-essential gatherings

2. Don't shake hands and hug others, especially people at higher risk of getting the COVID-19 virus (people above 65 years old and those in poor health)

Keep In Mind:

Physical Distancing is just one of the many ways to prevent COVID-19. You can also:

1. Wash your hands often for at least 20 seconds

2. Avoid touching your eyes, nose, and mouth

3. Cough or sneeze into the bend of your arm

4. Avoid touching surfaces that other people touch

How do I practice physical distancing?

Updated as of April 21, 2020

Here are the steps to create a 0.5% sodium hypochlorite (bleach) disinfectant solution (1:10 solution) for disinfection of surfaces:

1. Gather all materials needed. Work in a well-ventilated room, or outside in the shade, and protected from the wind.


2. Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, goggles, apron, and boots) when handling and preparing bleach solutions.


3. When preparing the solution, pour the amount of water required into a plastic container, then add bleach (and not the other way around).


4. If you are using household bleach (5% active chlorine), mix:

★ One mililiter (mL) bleach and nine mL water, or

★ 100 mL bleach and 900 mL water


5. If you are using chlorine powder, granules, or tablets (60 - 70% active chlorine), dissolve:

★ One tablespoon (tbsp) or 20 grams (g) chlorine and two liters (L) of water


6. Label your disinfectant container and specify the chlorine concentration.


7. Prepare bleach and water solutions fresh daily to preserve the solution's strength.

How do I make a disinfectant solution for handwashing?

Updated as of April 21, 2020

Here are the steps to create a 0.05% sodium hypochlorite (bleach) solution (1:100 solution) for hand-washing:

1. Use the 0.5% solution prepared for disinfecting surfaces. Mix one part of the surface disinfectant solution with nine parts water. For example:

★ Add 100 mililiters (mL) of the household bleach surface disinfectant solution to one liter (L) of water, or

★ Add one tablespoon of the chlorine powder, granule, or tablet surface disinfectant solution to 20L of water.


2. Use the handwashing solution with caution. Frequent use may lead to dermatitis, which could increase the risk of infection.

About COVID-19






What is COVID-19?

Updated on October 9, 2020

Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19)

Is an infectious disease caused by a new coronavirus recently introduced to humans for the first time. The World Health Organization (WHO) has called it a pandemic. This means that the virus has spread all over the world. About 80% of those who have COVID-19 have mild symptoms.


Coronaviruses are a large family of viruses causing a range of illnesses, from the common cold to more serious infections such as those caused by Middle East Respiratory Syndrome-related Coronavirus (MERS-CoV) and Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome-related Coronavirus (SARS-CoV). Coronavirus can also cause a variety of diseases in farm animals and domesticated pets.

What are the symptoms of COVID-19?

Updated on October 9, 2020

The most common symptoms are:

1. Fever (not necessarily a high fever)

2. Dry cough

3. Tiredness or fatigue

4. Shortness of breath or difficulty breathing

Patients may, likewise, experience other non-specific symptoms such as sore throat, nasal congestion, headache, diarrhea, nausea and vomiting, and loss of smell (anosmia) or loss of taste (ageusia) preceding the onset of respiratory symptoms.


Severe Symptoms include:

1. Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath

2. Persistent pain or pressure in the chest

3. Mental confusion or changes in alertness

4. Bluish lips or face


Symptoms of COVID-19 can range from mild to severe. It takes at most 14 days for symptoms of COVID-19 to show.

Some people are asymptomatic—they do not show symptoms, but they are infected and can transmit the virus.

If you have these AND live in a place under ECQ or have recently traveled to/from a place under ECQ, call your local BHERT or the DOH hotline - (02) 894 COVID (26843) or 1555 for all network subscribers. They will assess and advise you if you need to seek medical attention and which nearest health facility you should go to.

Who can get infected by the virus?

Updated on April 10, 2020

People of all ages can be infected with COVID-19.

Groups that are at higher risk of developing severe illness include:


1. People 65 years old and older (our lolos and lolas)

2. Anyone with pre-existing medical conditions (example: high blood pressure, heart or lung disease, or diabetes)

3. Those with high-risk pregnancies (example: women aged 17 and younger, aged 35 and older, those with pre-existing medical conditions)

How is COVID-19 transmitted?

Updated on April 10, 2020

1. Groups that are at higher risk of developing severe illness include:


2. COVID-19 is transmitted from person to person through droplets. It is transmitted when one individual talks, sneezes, or coughs producing ‘droplets’ of saliva containing the COVID-19 virus. These droplets are then inhaled by another person.


3. COVID-19 transmission usually occurs among close contacts -- including family members and healthcare workers. It is therefore important to maintain a distance of more than 1 meter away from any person who has respiratory symptoms.


4. A person can also catch COVID-19 by touching objects or surfaces, then touching their eyes, nose, or mouth. This is why it is important to regularly wash your hands.

What is a suspect, probable, and confirmed case?

Updated on April 21, 2020

What are the new classifications of individuals for COVID-19?

We have new classifications for our COVID-19 response:

1. Suspect;

2. Probable; and,

3. Confirmed.


PUIs will now be classified as suspect or probable. People who tested positive for COVID-19 will be classified as confirmed.


This is to align the Philippines' case classification with the definitions of the World Health Organizaiton (WHO) to have a more uniformed reporting mechanism across countries.



I have respiratory symptoms. Will I be classified as suspect?

You are defined as a person who is suspect with COVID-19 if:


1. You have an influenza-like illness (ILI) that manifests with fever (38°C), cough or sore throat, and have any of the following:


★ Travel history to an area with local transmission of COVID-19 during the 14 days before symptoms began; or,

★ History of close contact with person probable or confirmed with COVID-19 during the 14 days before symptoms began.


2. You have fever, cough, or other respiratory symptoms, and belong to vulnerable populations:


★ Those aged 60 years and above;

★ Those with pre-existing health conditions;

★ Those with high-risk pregnancies; and,

★ Health Workers


3. You developed severe and acuse respiratory illness with no explainable etiology that requires overnight hospitalization.


Who can be considered people probable with COVID-19?

A person is classified as probable with COVID-19 if the person was originally suspected with COVID-19 and their:


1. Test turned out inconclusive; or


2. Test was done in a non-official RT-PCR laboratory.


Who can be considered people confirmed with COVID-19?

A person is confirmed with COVID-19 if their samples sent for testing to official laboratories returned positive using the RT-PCR method.

Information for Everyone

Updated on April 10, 2020

Everyone can get COVID-19.

It’s important to always practice the following:

1. Wash hands with soap and water for 20 seconds or more (Try singing the chorus of your favorite song while washing)


2. Use a hand sanitizer with 70% alcohol if you can’t wash your hands for 20 seconds or more


3. Avoid touching your face, especially your mouth, eyes, nose


When you’re outside and going to public spaces, such as grocery, markets, drugstores, and banks:

1. Make sure you keep 1 meter distance from other people.


2. Avoid touching things that many people touch like door handles, tables, chairs, etc.


3. Wash your hands with soap and water after touching animals or animal products


4. Wear a mask while outside


Clean and disinfect

1. Frequently clean surfaces in your house, work or business. This includes tables, doorknobs, light switches, countertops, handles, desks, phones, keys, keyboards, toilets, faucets, and sinks.


2. Disinfect using a bleach solution (7 tablespoons [100 mL] bleach for 1 liter of clean water)


3. Disinfect your most used things (like phone, keys, eyeglasses, keyboard, etc.) with 70% alcohol solution

Information for Teens And Young Adults

Updated on April 10, 2020

You might be feeling sad or worried in a situation like this.


Know that you are not alone, and there are a lot of ways you can be a leader in keeping yourself, family, and community healthy:

1. Protect yourself by washing your hands frequently with soap and water for at least 20 seconds


2. Remember not to touch your face


3. Do not share cups, eating utensils, food or drinks with others


4. Share good practices such as hand washing and proper cough etiquette (eg. sneezing into your elbow) with family and friends


5. Don’t stigmatize or tease anyone about being sick as the virus chooses no one


6. Tell your parents, guardian, or caregiver if you feel sick


7. Practice social distancing and choose to stay home


8. Be a critical thinker and an active citizen in promoting facts about public health

Information for People at Risk

Updated on April 10, 2020

For Older People


You might be worried about the risk of getting sick. It is true that older people (age over 60) are at a higher risk of getting severe COVID-19 disease. But there are several ways you can protect yourself and loved ones.

1. When you have visitors, limit interaction to “1 meter greetings” - just wave or nod


2. Remind visitors and those you live with to practice frequent hand washing of at least 20 seconds


3. Regularly clean and disinfect surfaces at home, especially those that people touch a lot


4. Limit your shared spaces with people who aren’t feeling well


5. If you become sick with symptoms of COVID-19, call your local BHERT and doctor before visiting a healthcare facility.


6. Be ready with a household plan in case of a COVID-19 outbreak in your community


7. When you are out in public, follow the same preventive guidelines done at home. If possible, have someone else from your household go out instead of you.


8. Stay updated with information from reliable sources.

For Pregnant Women


You might be worried about your and your baby’s health. The latest data on COVID-19 show no increased risk to pregnant women and their unborn babies. But we know that changes in the body and immune system during pregnancy could make you vulnerable to respiratory infections.


Take extra care to protect yourself and your unborn child from COVID-19. Continue with your pre-natal check-ups as scheduled. When experiencing symptoms of COVID-19 or other illnesses, contact your local BHERT or health care provider immediately and follow their advice.


Here are some ways you can protect yourself and your unborn baby:

1. Protect yourself by washing your hands frequently with soap and water for at least 20 seconds


2. Practice social distancing of at least 6 feet, and avoid crowded spaces


3. Avoid touching your eyes, nose, and mouth


4. Practice proper cough etiquette, and dispose tissue immediately after use


5. If you have symptoms of fever, cough, or difficulty breathing, seek medical care immediately


6. Call a doctor by phone before visiting a healthcare facility


7. Non-urgent visits should be postponed


8. If your partner has COVID-19, or has symptoms, they should not go near you. They should also not go with you to the hospital.


9. There should be no accompanying person to outpatient visits unless really needed in patient care

For people with existing health conditions (like heart disease, diabetes, etc.)


You might feel anxious about getting sick given your underlying condition. Those at higher risk for severe COVID-19 disease include people with:

1. Chronic lung disease


2. Heart disease with complications


3. Immunocompromised including cancer treatment


4. Severe obesity (BMI > 40)


5. Underlying conditions that are not well controlled like diabetes, renal failure, or liver disease


While you may be worried, there are a lot of ways you can protect yourself and others:

1. When you have visitors, limit interaction to “1 meter greetings” - wave or nod


2. Remind visitors and those you live with to practice frequent hand washing of at least 20 seconds


3. Regularly clean and disinfect surfaces at home, especially those that people touch a lot


4. Limit your shared spaces with people who aren’t feeling well


5. If you become sick with symptoms of COVID 19, call your local BHERT and doctor before visiting a healthcare facility.


6. Be ready with a household plan in case of a COVID-19 outbreak in your community


7. When you are out in public, follow the same preventive guidelines done at home


8. Stay updated with information from reliable sources

Twenty One Pilots Tribute

Responsive Web Design

This tribute page was created for a FreeCodeCamp project. The goal was very simple: make a one-page site with images, text, and external links. I took this chance to practice using the Bootstrap 3 framework to create a tribute to one of my favorite bands at the moment, Twenty One Pilots (TOP). Lucky for me, they have such strong branding that the page nearly designed itself!

I kept the color palette simple with black, white, and the signature TOP shade of red for the accent color. To add some visual interest, I used a subtle twill texture for the red backgrounds and styled the headings with a black background to give it a label maker tape effect. I also wrote some code that replaces the letter "O" with the scandinavian letter "Ø" in the headings and navigation links to match the TOP logo.

Project Details

Too Much Sauce

Responsive Web Design

Too Much Sauce (TMS) is a side project of motion graphic designer Ryan Bechtel, where he challenges himself to create brutalist designs. The purpose of the TMS website is to host his collection of animated GIFs, which were originally shared as Instagram stories.

Ryan really liked the bold, high-contrast look of Complex.com, a music and pop culture media/news website, so that's what inspired the design of this project. The color palette is mainly black and white with splashes of magenta and neon green from the TMS brand. It was built with the latest version of Bootstrap 4 and uses a small JavaScript library, Gifffer.js, to prevent the animated GIFs from autoplaying when the page loads.

Project Details

Ryan Bechtel

Responsive Web Design

This project was for Ryan Bechtel, a motion graphic designer in Chicago, IL. His main goal was to attract prospective clients for freelance jobs, so he wanted a website that showcases some of his best work.

I kept the design of this website simple to help Ryan's work stand out. His reel (AKA a video portfolio) is the most important piece of content, probably the first thing his future clients would look for, so it is placed front and center on the landing page. To make the embedded videos responsive and looking good on all devices, I used a jQuery plugin called FitVids.js, which gives the videos a fluid width. For the contact form, I used PHPMailer, an email sending library for PHP, to make it functional and also coded in a "honeypot" to help prevent spam. Other than the two plugins I just mentioned, the rest of this website was completely hand-coded.
